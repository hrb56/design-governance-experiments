COMM 4940: Syllabus

Lectures: 8:40am - 9:50am Monday and Wednesdays

Location: Kennedy Hall 213

Size: 24-student limit

Pre-requisites: Statistics or research methods coursework

S/U: No

Audit: No, since this course is a project-based course

Office Hours: 10am - 11:30am Mondays and Wednesdays
    486 Mann Library (look for the LEGO door sign) (sign up here or drop in) (read Cornell's student guide to office hours)

Weekly Activities: Students will read the assigned material, complete a weekly writing or analysis challenge in pairs, and discuss reading on Slack. The course final is a group project and a short paper.

Grading: Precept participation: 20%. Weekly assignments: 20%. Midterm experiment design: 20%. Final project: 40%.

Weekly Assignments: Due 9pm on Tuesdays

Pre-requisites: POL 345/SOC 301, or with permission of the instructor, other background in statistics or data analysis.

Meeting topics and assigned readings

Lecture sessions will alternate between lectures & discussions of key issues that sometimes include student-led components. Each week will also include workshops that focus on technical topics, collaboration skill-building, and project feedback.

This syllabus is a living document, and the readings are subject to change as we go along, so please keep checking this document for the latest.

Part I: Understanding Field Experiments

Lecture (Wed Jan 22) Field Experiments in Policy, Products, and Social Science
  • This lecture will introduce the class and include space for Q&A about the class and its structure.
  • Individual assignment: complete the 5 minute intro survey by Friday, Jan 24 at 5pm
Lecture & Discussion (Mon Jan 27) What is Causal Knowledge and Why Does it Matter?
  • Why do we care about cause and effect, what does it even mean, and how is the knowledge used? In this class, we will discuss the reasons that researchers and companies do experiments in society.
  • Reading before class:
    • Linden, G. (2006). Early Amazon: Shopping cart recommendations. Geeking with Greg.
    • William R. Shadish, Thomas D. Cook, and Donald T. Campbell. Experimental and Quasi-Experimental Designs for Generalized Causal Inference. Boston: Houghton-Mifflin, 2002. (read chapter 1 up to "Modern Descriptions of Experiments")
  • Important Trainings:
    • (required) Students should plan to complete Cornell's IRB training. Make sure to plan this into your week. IRB training can take 3-6 hours to complete, and you can space it out over several sessions if you need to.
    • (optional) If you haven't used R before, please sign up for Cornell's workshop on Introductory Statistical Analysis Using R (details here), which is available on February 10th and 17th 2020.
Workshop (Wed Jan 29) How Experiments Work
  • This workshop session reviews basic concepts needed for the class and introduces the process of conducting a randomized trial, including treatments, random assignment, and outcomes. Students will also be introduced to the notations and conventions used in the class.
  • Individual assignment (Due Tuesday Feb 4 at 9pm):
    • Facebook Poem Experiment: Write a four-paragraph essay reporting your results from an experiment that has already been conducted. Full details are on github.
Lecture & Discussion (Mon Feb 3)  Studying Online Behavior at Scale
  • Fitts, Alexis Sobel. (2014) The king of content: How Upworthy aims to alter the Web, and could end up altering the world. Columbia Journalism Review.
  • Kohavi, R., Deng, A., Frasca, B., Walker, T., Xu, Y., & Pohlmann, N. (2013). Online controlled experiments at large scale. In Proceedings of the 19th ACM SIGKDD international conference on Knowledge discovery and data mining (pp. 1168–1176). ACM.
Workshop (Wed Feb 5) Designing an Experiment Together
  • Guest: Becca Rodomsky-Bish, Cornell Lab of Ornithology
  • During February, the class will be designing and running an experiment together with the Cornell Lab of Ornithology. In this session, we will learn more about the Great Backyard Bird Count (GBBC) (which happens Feb 14 - 17) and discuss an follow-up intervention with over the project's 160,000 contributors that could inspire further involvement in citizen science.
  • Reading before class:
    • Wallaert, Matthew. (2019) How to Design for Behavioral Change. Product Tank. (24:36 video)
  • Pair Assignment (due Tuesday Feb 11 at 9pm):
    • Read through examples of last year's follow-up emails
    • Create a list of possible ⬆️ promoting pressures and ⬇️ inhibiting pressures for someone to open the email, click a link, and sign up for a further project.
    • Create a new email that you think will be more influential. Submit this example email in PDF form. You can create it using any software you prefer, including a word processor like Word or Pages or even presentation software like Powerpoint or Keynote. When doing this, consider:
      • Changing the headline
      • Changing the text of the email
      • Changing the layout (the order or size of key elements)
    • Write no more than 3 paragraphs listing each pressures you chose to influence, how you think your changes might influence them, and how confident you are on a scale of 1 to 10.
    • Submit your list, your proposed email, and your explanation as your assignment by Feb 11.
    • West, S. E., & Pateman, R. M. (2016). Recruiting and retaining participants in citizen science: What can be learned from the volunteering literature?. Citizen Science: Theory and Practice. (pay particular attention to the section on sustained participation)
Lecture & Discussion (Mon Feb 10) Experiments as Listening,  Manipulation, or Both?
  • When organizations conduct A/B tests, are they influencing people, manipulating them, or just listening to people's revealed preferences? In this class, we will discuss this question.
  • Reading:
    • Scheiber, N. (2017). How Uber uses psychological tricks to push its drivers’ buttons. The New York Times, 2.
    • Karpf, D. (2016). Analytic activism: Digital listening and the new political strategy (chapter four). Oxford University Press. (the PDF is in the course site)
    • Pair Assignment (due Tuesday Feb 16 at 9pm):
      • With you partner, pick an experiment that you have learned about in class, read about in the news, or even the experiment with the GBBC. Each of you write an essay with five short paragraphs (3-4 sentences) arguing your side. Then swap essays, read each others' arguments and give each other feedback before submitting your essays together.
      • On the side of manipulation, you might find it helpful to review section II: "What manipulation is" from:
        • Sunstein, C. R. (2015). Fifty shades of manipulation. Journal of Behavioral Marketing
Workshop (Wed Feb 12) Analyzing & Interpreting Experiment Results
  • In this workshop, we will:
    • Review questions remaining from the first assignment
    • Discuss the data collected for the GBBC
    • Discuss our plan for analyzing data from the GBBC
  • Students should have completed Cornell's IRB training by this point in the class. Make sure to plan this into your week. IRB training can take as many as six hours to complete
Lecture & Discussion (Mon Feb 17) Research Ethics
  • How do US regulations govern social research in universities? Should corporate experiments be similarly regulated?
  • We will also start discussing the final class project
  • Reading before class:
    • Desposato, S. (2014, November 3). Ethics and research in comparative politics. Washington Post.
    • Grimmelmann, J. (2015). The law and ethics of experiments on social media users. 13 Colo. Tech. L.J. 219, 2015
Workshop (Wed Feb 19) Planning an Experiment (Components & Assumptions of Experiment Design)
  • This workshop introduces core components of any experiment design and summarizes core assumptions of experiment design, including excludability and non-interference.
  • We will be joined by Becca Rodomsky-Bish to review finalize our GBBC interventions

Part II: Planning Class Project

Workshop (Wed Feb 26) Planning An Experiment (Outcomes, Power Analysis)
  • In this workshop, students learn how to choose outcome variables and conduct a power analysis starting with observed behavior, projecting possible effects, and simulating the chance of observing the effect for a given study design
  • This workshop will also include an introduction to the software platform used for statistical analysis throughout the class
  • Reading:
    • Lee, Stephanie M. (2018) Sliced & Diced: The Inside Story of How an Ivy League Food Scientist Turned Shoddy Data into Viral Studies. Buzzfeed
    • Pettingill, L. M. (2017, March 21). 4 Principles for Making Experimentation Count. Airbnb Engineering and Data Science.
Lecture & Discussion (Mon March 2nd) Replication & Meta-Analysis: Does It Work More Than Once?
  • In this lecture, we will discuss how researchers conduct replications and combine the results of studies to confirm that ideas work more than once
  • As you work toward forming teams, we will also discuss effective collaboration practices in teams
  • Reading:
    • Bavel, J. V. (2016, May 27). Why Do So Many Studies Fail to Replicate? The New York Times.
    • Chalwa, Dalmeet Singh. (2017, Nov 8) A new ‘accelerator’ aims to bring big science to psychology. Science Magazine
Workshop (Wed March 4th) Introducing the Upworthy Archive
  • In this workshop, we will discuss the Upworthy Archive and possibilities for class projects in greater detail
  • Reading:
    • Matias, J. N. (2019) Announcing the Upworthy Research Archive. Medium
  • Assignment (team assignment Due Tues March 10 at 9pm): Submit an initial project proposal draft
Lecture & Discussion (Mon March 9th) Why Scientists Do Field Experiments
  • So far, we have discussed the pragmatic value of field experiments. Why do scientists do them?
  • Reading:
    • Mortensen, C. R., & Cialdini, R. B. (2010). Full-Cycle Social Psychology for Theory and Application. Social and Personality Psychology Compass, 4 (1), 53–63.
    • Gantman, A., Brady, W., Bavel, J.V. (2019) Why Moral Emotions Go Viral Online. Scientific American.
Lecture & Discussion (Wed March 11) Clickbait & Viral Content
  • Upworthy was accused of popularizing clickbait. What is clickbait, and what are its effects?
  • Reading:
    • Sanders, S. (2017). Upworthy was one of the hottest sites ever. You won’t believe what happened next. NPR
    • Munger, K., Luca, M., Nagler, J., & Tucker, J. (2018). The Effect of Clickbait.
  • Assignment (team assignment Due Tues March 24 at 9pm): Submit an initial project proposal
Lecture & Discussion (Mon March 16) The Role of Experiments in a Democracy
  • Readings:
    • Matias, J. N. (2016, December 12). The Obligation To Experiment. MIT Media Lab.
    • Thaler, R. H., & Sunstein, C. R. (2003). Libertarian paternalism. American economic review, 93(2), 175-179.
Workshop (Wed March 18) Combining Results from Experiments: Meta-Analysis
  • This workshop, will introduce statistical methods for analyzing combined results from experiments.
Lecture & Discussion ( Mon March 23) How Organizations Learn from A/B Tests
  • Do people and companies actually learn and become more effective by doing AB tests?
  • Readings TBD
Midterm Due (March 24, 9pm) Class Project Proposal 
  • The midterm for the class will be to submit a collective proposal with your team for what your class project will be.
Lecture & Discussion ( Wed March 25) Interpreting, Using, and Misusing Experiment Results
  • Readings:
    • Weiss, C. H. (1979). The many meanings of research utilization. Public Administration Review, 39 (5), 426–431.
    • Koerth-Baker, M. (2017, May 11). The Tangled Story Behind Trump’s False Claims Of Voter Fraud.
